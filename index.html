Thermal


 

LexJet’s thermal laminates provide high clarity, increased bond strength, superior resistance to abrasion, water, oil, acid and alkali and are perfect for high-production runs on bond inkjet paper, microporous-coated inkjet paper, electrostatic and continuous tone photographic prints.

Compatibility:

  • When thermal laminating, you want to use a dimensionally stable media
  • Thermal laminates will not stick consistently to swellable coatings
  • Thermal laminates work perfectly on any microporous coated paper/polyester
  • Thermal laminates will work on hybrid coatings, but not as good as microporous
  • Thermal laminates will work on macroporous coatings, but many macroporous coated products are not good to thermal laminate as they are vinyl

Suggested Settings:

  • Pressure — 20-40 psi
  • Speed — 2-3 feet/min
  • Tension — Yes (no slack in film)
  • Heat — 205° F
  • Heat Seal Initiation — 170° F (78° C) ½ sec. dwell @ 40 psi

Liquid

A liquid laminate can be a great alternative to thermal or pressure sensitive adhesive and is effective for protecting digital prints, fine art, signs, banners and other products from the elements. Liquid laminates produce little waste and are extremely economical when machine-applied. The coating process offers as much or more UV-and abrasion-protection as film laminates. They also stretch and conform to uneven surfaces.

Suggested Settings:

  • Speed — 3 feet/min
  • Heat — 100° F

FAQ

What is a Laminate?

A laminate is a film with either a thermal or PSA activated adhesive, designed to go on top or back (or both) of the graphic and serves 4 functions:

  • Protect the Graphic
  • Enhance the appearance or finish
  • Add body, thickness, or rigidity
  • Specialty Applications

Why use a Laminate?

The three main reasons to use a laminate are to protect the graphic, enhance the appearance, and to add rigidity.

Protecting the graphic

  • Moisture resistance
  • UV inhibitors (in adhesive)
  • Fading
  • Scratching
  • Climatic radiation (rain, hail, snow, etc)
  • Fingerprints
  • Graffiti
  • Vandalism

Enhancing the Appearance

  • 4 basic finishes:
    • Gloss
    • Luster (satin, semi-matte or semi-gloss)
    • Matte
    • Velvet/Textured
  • The finish depends on the application
  • Luster is the most widely used, followed by matte, then gloss
  • Velvet/Textured are used because of their non-glare properties, as well as hiding fingerprints and scratches

Adding Rigidity

  • Increase rigidity for handling, mounting, and shipping
  • Rigidity is required when making tradeshow graphics because the graphic needs body to hang properly
  • Flexibility is required because the graphics need to be rolled and unrolled
  • For graphics that will be rolled, the laminate, and backing film combination are critical

Understanding Dry Time, Cure Time, and Cutting

  • Ink Drying Time
    • It’s important for the inks to be completely dry before laminating. A good way to know if the print is ready to be laminated is to make sure it is dry to the touch. If the ink is still tacky, allow more dry time. Printer manufacturers recommend waiting 24 hours.
  • Adhesive Cure Time
    • It is important to allow ample time for your adhesives to cure before trimming and rolling your graphics. Insufficient cure time can lead to poor bond strength, causing tunneling, delaminating, and edge lift. We recommend allowing at least 2 hours for the adhesive to cure before trimming and rolling your graphics. A full 24 hours of cure time will yield the best bond.
  • Cutting & Trimming
    • When trimming graphics, if possible, you should always cut through the softer material first. Be sure to use a freshly sharpened blade and trim the graphics with a straight edge on tempered glass. This will yield the most consistent results.

Understanding Laminate Base Films

  • The base film is critical when selecting a laminate to work properly with your printable media.
  • The most common types of base films are:
    • Polyester
      • Rigid, dimensionally stable
      • Regular and hard coat
    • Vinyl
      • Softer, more flexible, less dimensionally stable
    • Polycarbonate
      • Tough, very expensive, generally thicker
      • Known by GE brand name Lexan
    • Polypropylene
      • Inexpensive, more stable than vinyl
      • Poor performance with UV light and severe temps compared to vinyl
    • Coex
      • A mixture of vinyl and polyester, resulting in a stable film that is soft to cut, yet durable to scratching

What are PSA Laminates?

  • Referred to as cold laminates (80+ degrees preferred)
  • Applied by pressure and do not require heat
  • Consist of many different film types, many different types of adhesives
  • Release liner
  • Like tape – It’s sticky to the touch and the more pressure, the better it sticks
  • PSAs are less durable, except for certain ink jet applications
  • Create a mechanical bond rather than a chemical bond
  • Initial cost of product is more expensive than thermal

What Are Thermal or Low-melt Laminates?

  • Heat activated and applied by pressure
  • Bond occurs during the cooling process
  • Creates a chemical bond
  • Consists of polyester or PVC film with a co-polymer adhesive such as polyethylene
  • Temperature range is 165° to 290° F
  • No release liner
  • Initial cost of product is least expensive
